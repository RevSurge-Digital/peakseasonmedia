Restaurant Websites That Turn Lookers Into Bookers

Fast, mobile-first sites for Gulf Coast food businesses — menus, hours and reservations where a hungry person can actually find them, and built so Google and AI assistants can read every word.

Your Site Has One Job

A restaurant website is not a brochure. It is the last thirty seconds between a hungry person and a reservation, and most of them are lost in those thirty seconds — to a menu PDF that won't open on a phone, an hours block that still says “closed for renovation,” or a reservation button four taps deep.

We build sites that answer the four questions every diner actually has, above the fold, on a phone, in under two seconds: what do you serve, are you open, where are you, and how do I book. Everything else — the story, the press, the gallery — lives below that line where it belongs.

Design comes second to speed here, and speed is not a preference. Google ranks on Core Web Vitals, and a diner standing on Main Street at 7 p.m. on one bar of signal will not wait four seconds for a hero video to load. Our builds ship as fast static pages with real HTML menus, not embedded images of menus that neither Google nor an AI assistant can read.

A menu locked inside a PDF is invisible to Google, to ChatGPT, and to anyone using a screen reader.

Built to be found

Every page ships with the structured data that puts your hours, menu, price range and reviews into Google's results and into the answers AI assistants give. It is the same work that makes the site accessible — machines and screen readers want the same thing: clean, labelled, real text.

Built to be updated

Seasonal menus change, and a site you have to email someone to update is a site that goes stale by March. You get a simple editor for the things that actually change — hours, menu items, specials, events — and we handle the rest.

The Website Playbook

  • Mobile-first builddesigned on a 390px phone, then widened
  • Real HTML menusreadable by Google, AI assistants & screen readers
  • Booking front and centerOpenTable, Resy, Toast or your own form

The site plugs straight into the other engines: restaurant advertising needs landing pages that convert, and local SEO & AEO needs a site Google and ChatGPT can actually read.

What a Rebuild Usually Fixes

Most of the sites we replace are not ugly. They are slow, unreadable to machines, and organised around what the owner wanted to say rather than what the diner came to find. The measurable wins are almost always the same four: a mobile page that loads in under two seconds instead of five, a menu Google can index, a booking path that takes one tap instead of four, and tracking that finally proves which channel earned the reservation.

We scope a rebuild the way we scope everything else — against your numbers. If your current site converts well and just needs its menus freed and its speed fixed, we will tell you that instead of selling you a rebuild. It is a small coast; reputations are the only currency that survives the off-season.

Good questions

Questions Operators Ask Us

How long does a restaurant website take to build?

Most single-location builds run four to six weeks from kickoff to launch: a week on structure and content, two to three weeks on build and menu work, then a review and launch week. The long pole is almost never the design — it is getting current menus, hours and photography out of your head and into a file. We start that on day one so it is not what holds up launch in week five.

Can you keep our existing branding?

Yes, and often we should. If your logo, colours and photography are working, a rebuild is about speed, structure and findability, not a new look. We will tell you honestly whether the brand is holding you back or whether the site is simply built badly underneath a perfectly good brand.

Do you handle hosting and updates?

We host on a fast static platform with SSL, backups and a global CDN included, and we handle the technical updates. Day-to-day content — hours, menu items, specials, events — you can edit yourself in a simple editor, because a menu you have to email someone to change is a menu that will be wrong by March.

Will the site be accessible?

Every build targets WCAG 2.1 AA: real text instead of images of text, colour contrast that passes, keyboard-operable navigation, labelled form fields and proper heading structure. It is the right thing to do, it reduces legal exposure, and it happens to be exactly what makes a site readable to Google and to AI assistants.
